Why Causality Matters

The Question That Statistics Refused to Ask
Pearl · Intro & Ch.1 Gallea · Ch.1
"You cannot answer a question that you cannot ask, and you cannot ask a question you have no words for." — Pearl Both authors open with the same conviction: across science, policy, business, and personal life, the most important questions are causal — and for most of modern history, we lacked the tools to answer them.
1
The Case for Causal Thinking
◆ TWO AUTHORS, ONE DIAGNOSIS
PEARL'S DIAGNOSIS (THEORETICAL)
Science has always asked "why" — but statistics, its main tool, spent a century banning causal language. The result: a gap between what scientists wanted to know and what their methods could tell them. Data alone cannot tell us what causes what.
GALLEA'S DIAGNOSIS (PRACTICAL)
People make billion-dollar decisions based on opinions and correlations, not causal evidence. Did Trump's policies or COVID reduce immigration? Did your marketing campaign or the weather boost sales? Without causal tools, we cannot know — and wrong attribution leads to wrong decisions.
◆ THE COST OF IGNORING CAUSALITY
HEALTH
Misinformation about drug efficacy — wasted money, delayed treatment. Premature claims about COVID cures.
POLICY
Trump claimed his policies caused lowest immigration — but it was January 2021, mid-COVID. Wrong attribution → wrong future policies.
BUSINESS
Streaming service changes homepage → subscriptions fall. But competitor launched + sunny month. Did the team cause the fall, or prevent worse?
ENVIRONMENT
Replace petrol car with electric → drive more, stop walking → net emissions may increase. Rebound effects hide in causal chains.
2
The Ladder of Causation
◆ PEARL'S CENTRAL FRAMEWORK — THREE LEVELS OF CAUSAL REASONING
Pearl organizes all causal questions into three rungs. Each rung requires strictly more powerful reasoning than the one below. Gallea's entire book operates within this same hierarchy — his 5-step framework, RCTs, and quasi-experiments are all tools for climbing from rung 1 to rungs 2 and 3.
3
Dumb Data vs. Biased Minds
◆ THE DILEMMA BOTH AUTHORS IDENTIFY
Pearl and Gallea converge on the same paradox. Data has no sense of causality — it merely records numbers. But our minds are riddled with biases that distort causal judgment. The solution: a formal framework that combines domain knowledge with data, each compensating for the other's weakness.
DATA IS "DUMB" (PEARL)
The causal model is not in the data — it is brought to the data by the researcher. No amount of data can tell you whether X causes Y or Y causes X. A model encoding your assumptions about the world's mechanisms is indispensable.
WE ARE BIASED (GALLEA)
Confirmation bias: we seek evidence confirming beliefs. Availability bias: loud voices distort perceived frequencies. Apophenia: we see patterns in pure noise. Africa has more sun but fewer solar panels — domain knowledge reveals hidden confounders (heat, income).
THE SHARED SOLUTION: Pearl calls it the "Causal Inference Engine" — a model (your assumptions) plus data (your observations) feeding into a formal reasoning system. Gallea calls it the "Causal Mindset" — a disciplined habit of asking the right questions before trusting data.
4
What Is Causal Inference?
◆ TWO COMPLEMENTARY DEFINITIONS
PEARL'S DEFINITION
A mathematical framework with three pillars: causal diagrams (DAGs), the do-operator, and counterfactual logic. Together they answer: What would happen if we intervened? What would have happened otherwise? Who is responsible for an outcome?
GALLEA'S DEFINITION
The scientific field providing tools to: (1) assess causality from past events (backward-looking), and (2) predict outcomes of future choices (forward-looking) — using data + domain knowledge + rigorous methods. Two roles: forensic detective and fortune teller.
BACKWARD
"Did the marketing campaign work?"
FORWARD
"What happens if we change the price?"
COUNTERFACTUAL
"Would the patient have survived otherwise?"
5
The Firing Squad — One Story, Three Questions
◆ PEARL'S CANONICAL EXAMPLE MEETS GALLEA'S CASE-STUDY METHOD
A prisoner is executed by two soldiers on a captain's command, who acts on a court order. Either soldier's shot is sufficient to kill. The same diagram generates three progressively harder questions — one for each rung.
RUNG 1
Observation
"If Soldier A fired, what is the probability the prisoner is dead?"
Very high — but Soldier B also fired. This is mere correlation.
RUNG 2
Intervention
"What if Soldier A had decided NOT to fire?"
The prisoner still dies — because Soldier B fired. Intervention analysis reveals redundancy.
RUNG 3
Counterfactual
"Was Soldier A's shot a necessary cause of death?"
No — the prisoner would have died anyway from B. But it WAS a sufficient cause. Legal reasoning needs this distinction.
GALLEA'S PARALLEL: His case studies follow the same diagnostic logic — asking whether an observed correlation could have alternative explanations. The cold shower question is a rung-2 question that most people answer with rung-1 evidence.
6
Why This Matters Now
◆ THE CAUSAL REVOLUTION'S PROMISE
Drug Development
Move beyond 'correlates with survival' to 'causes survival, via this mechanism, in this population.'
AI & ML
Build systems that explain decisions, learn from interventions, and reason about counterfactuals — not just predict from patterns.
Business & Policy
Gallea's core promise: stop making billion-dollar decisions from correlations. Build the habit of asking 'would this outcome have happened anyway?'
Legal & Ethical
Pearl's formalism assigns probability estimates to whether a defendant's action was a necessary or sufficient cause of harm.

The Language of Cause & Effect

Diagrams, Junctions, and the Fundamental Problem
Pearl · Ch.3 Gallea · Ch.3
"Bayesian networks were my personal route into causality — and ultimately the tool I had to transcend." — PearlPearl provides the formal building blocks: Bayes's rule, three junction types, and the leap from probability to causality. Gallea reveals the deeper conceptual challenge: why proving causality is fundamentally impossible without the right assumptions — the "parallel worlds" problem.
1
The Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference
◆ WHY CAUSALITY CAN NEVER BE DIRECTLY OBSERVED
Gallea names the deepest challenge: to know that X caused Y, you'd need to observe the same individual both with and without X — simultaneously. This is physically impossible. You can only observe one world.
THE FUNDAMENTAL PROBLEM
Causal effect = (outcome with treatment) − (outcome without treatment). But you can only ever observe one of these. The counterfactual is unobservable. Every causal claim is therefore an inference, not a direct observation.
THE SOLUTION
Assumptions + data + formal models. Either run an experiment (RCT), find natural experiments, or use observational data with a causal model specifying all assumptions. The assumptions must be transparent and defensible.
2
Bayes's Rule — The Foundation
◆ UPDATING BELIEFS WITH EVIDENCE
P(Cause | Effect) = P(Effect | Cause) × P(Cause) / P(Effect)
Bayes's rule is the calculus of belief-updating: prior belief × likelihood of evidence → posterior belief. Pearl built his entire Bayesian network framework on this. It is the engine inside causal reasoning.
KEY INSIGHT: The same data can be compatible with multiple causal structures. Bayes's rule helps us update our belief about which structure is correct — but it cannot, alone, determine causation. That requires additional causal assumptions.
3
From Bayesian Networks to Causal Diagrams
◆ ONE CRUCIAL CHANGE IN INTERPRETATION
Bayesian Network
Arrows = conditional probability relationships (no direction claim). P(Y | X) only — stuck on Rung 1. Cannot distinguish seeing from doing.
Causal Diagram (DAG)
Arrows = direct causal effect ("X listens to Y before choosing"). P(Y | do(X)) answers — Rungs 1, 2, and 3. Machinery from BN fully reusable.
PEARL'S APOSTASY: He built Bayesian networks believing they were the key to AI. By the late 1980s he realized he was wrong and pivoted entirely to causality. His students found this "shocking." This intellectual honesty led to the Causal Revolution and the 2011 Turing Award.
4
Three Junctions — Building Blocks of All Diagrams
◆ EVERY CAUSAL DIAGRAM IS MADE OF THESE THREE PATTERNS
Pearl shows that any DAG can be analyzed by examining its three-node junctions. Gallea identifies the same structures as mediators (chain), confounders (fork), and colliders. Click each to explore.
COLLIDERS ARE MOST COUNTERINTUITIVE: They create associations where none existed. Controlling for a collider is one of the most common and dangerous errors in applied research — it introduces confounding rather than removing it.
5
Choosing the Right Counterfactual
◆ GALLEA'S CASE STUDIES — WHY THE COMPARISON GROUP MATTERS
Once you understand the fundamental problem, you realize that every causal claim implicitly assumes a comparison to something that didn't happen. Gallea shows how choosing the wrong counterfactual can flip your conclusions entirely.
E-Scooter Emissions
Assumed scooters replace cars.
Research shows scooters actually replace walking & transit — which are lower-emission. Service likely INCREASED net emissions.
Customer Loyalty Card
Compare card vs. non-members.
Selection bias: frequent shoppers self-select into loyalty programs. Non-members are fundamentally different pre-program.
GenAI Therapy
Compare to: nothing, therapy, or web support?
Study used wait-list control → valid for 'can AI fill the gap?' but NOT for 'is AI better than therapy?'

Confounders, Colliders & Diagnosing Bias

The do-Operator Meets the Diagnostic Eye
Pearl · Ch.4 Gallea · Ch.4
"Confounding is not a statistical notion. It stands for the discrepancy between what we want to assess and what we actually do assess using statistical methods." — PearlPearl provides the formal theory: the do-operator and back-door criterion. Gallea provides the practical diagnostic eye: three questions that expose confounders, reverse causation, and external validity threats in any real-world claim.
1
Defining Confounding — Finally
◆ A CENTURY OF MISDEFINITION — CORRECTED
Pre-1990s: 'Third variable'
Circular — requires knowing which variables are 'relevant'. No criterion.
Epidemiology: three-part test
Incorrectly classifies colliders as confounders.
Greenland & Robins (1986)
Conceptually correct but unoperationalizable without diagrams.
Pearl: P(Y|X) ≠ P(Y|do(X))
Precise, operational, connected to causal diagrams. Confounding = observation ≠ intervention.
2
The do-Operator
◆ FORMALIZING THE GAP BETWEEN SEEING AND DOING
The do-operator surgically intervenes on a variable: sets X to a value while severing all incoming arrows. This is what a randomized experiment does physically — and what causal diagrams allow us to simulate mathematically.
FORMAL DEFINITION: X and Y are confounded if P(Y | X) ≠ P(Y | do(X)). Confounding exists whenever a back-door path from X to Y is open. Gallea's Ch.6 (RCT) works precisely because randomization is equivalent to applying the do-operator: it severs all back-door paths by design.
3
The Back-Door Criterion
◆ DECONFOUNDING AS A PUZZLE GAME
Chain A→B→C
Control for B blocks the path. Don't control if you want total effect.
Fork A←B→C
Control for B blocks confounding. B is a confounder.
Collider A→B←C
DON'T control for B. Opens a spurious path.
Descendant
Controlling partially opens the collider. Treat like controlling for the collider itself.
THE "CONTROL FOR EVERYTHING" FALLACY: Naively including all variables as controls is wrong and can introduce more bias than it removes. Any collider controlled for opens a spurious association.
◆ FOUR DECONFOUNDING PUZZLES — WHERE INTUITION FAILS
4
Gallea's Three Diagnostic Questions
◆ THE PRACTITIONER'S FORENSIC TOOLKIT
Where Pearl gives you the algorithm, Gallea gives you the mindset. Three questions to ask whenever you encounter a causal claim — the qualitative version of Pearl's formal criteria.
5
The Adjustment Formula
◆ COMPUTING CAUSAL EFFECTS FROM OBSERVATIONAL DATA
P(Y | do(X)) = Σz P(Y | X, Z=z) × P(Z=z)
Compute the effect within each stratum of Z, then average across Z's distribution
WHEN IT WORKS
You have data on a sufficient set Z satisfying the back-door criterion. Works for any functional form (not just linear).
WHEN IT FAILS
Required deconfounders are unobservable. Then front-door adjustment or instrumental variables may still work (Chapter 7).
6
Case Studies — Gallea's Forensic Analysis
◆ APPLYING BOTH FRAMEWORKS TO REAL CLAIMS
Chocolate & Nobel Laureates (NEJM)
GDP confounds both. Classic fork: Wealth → Chocolate and Wealth → Research funding. Pearl: control for GDP closes the back-door path.
Police & Crime
Reverse causation. Police deployed TO high-crime areas. Arrow goes Crime → Police, not Police → Crime. Pearl: the DAG disambiguates direction.
COVID Lockdowns & Deaths
Reverse causation + confounders. Lockdowns imposed WHERE deaths are rising. Comparing regions with/without lockdowns is confounded by severity. Need quasi-experimental methods (Ch.7).
Cold Swimming & Longevity
Selection bias. Healthy people self-select into cold swimming. The 'treatment group' is fundamentally different before treatment even begins. Conditioning on health status (collider descendant) could make it worse.

Gallea's 5-Step Causal Mindset Framework

2
Historical Validation: The Smoking-Cancer Debate
◆ PEARL'S CASE STUDY — WHERE GALLEA'S FRAMEWORK WAS NEEDED MOST
The smoking debate is Pearl's chosen case study because it shows, in maximum clarity, what happens when science lacks causal vocabulary. The evidence was overwhelming by the mid-1950s. But without formal tools, the debate dragged 15 years while millions smoked.
THE CATCH-22
RCT = impossible (can't assign people to smoke for decades). Observational data = dismissed as "only statistical" by Fisher and tobacco industry. Without formal causal tools, science was left arguing from data alone.
THE IRONY
Fisher was right that correlation doesn't prove causation. But he was wrong that the hypothesis couldn't be confirmed. Cornfield showed that causal reasoning about confounders — not just more data — was the way forward.
3
Cornfield's Inequality
◆ SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS BEFORE ITS TIME
Jerome Cornfield fought Fisher on Fisher's own turf: mathematics. He showed that for a "smoking gene" to fully explain the 9× cancer risk, it would need to make smokers 9× more likely to carry the gene — biologically inconceivable. This is Gallea's Step 3 ("Direction of bias?") formalized as a mathematical bound.
If observed risk ratio = 9, then any confounder must have a prevalence ratio ≥ 9
Cornfield's inequality: the confounder must be at least as strong as the observed association to fully explain it away
LEGACY: Now called sensitivity analysis — instead of assuming no confounding, ask: how strong would a confounder have to be? If the required strength is implausible, the causal claim stands. This is now formalized with causal diagrams.
4
Hill's Nine Criteria
◆ SCIENCE'S FIRST INFORMAL CAUSAL FRAMEWORK
The 1964 Surgeon General's report used Bradford Hill's nine criteria to build the causal case without an RCT. These are the intellectual ancestors of Gallea's 5-step framework.
GALLEA'S ADVANCE: Hill's criteria are informal and require expert judgment. Gallea's 5-step framework operationalizes the same logic for non-experts, while Pearl's back-door criterion provides the formal algorithm that removes ambiguity entirely.

When Intuition Fails

Paradoxes, Wrong Metrics, and Goodhart's Law
Pearl · Ch.6 Gallea · Ch.8

Why Paradoxes Matter

WITH CAUSAL DIAGRAMS

2

◆ ONE DIAGRAM RESOLVES THEM ALL

3
Gallea's Twist: Wrong Question
◆ EVEN PERFECT CAUSAL METHODS FAIL IF THE QUESTION IS WRONG
Gallea introduces a complementary failure mode that Pearl's paradoxes don't cover: what happens when the causal method is impeccable, but the outcome you're measuring doesn't capture what you actually care about?
MISUNDERSTOOD PATHWAY (The 'How?' Problem)
You prove Drug X reduces blood pressure. But blood pressure was a proxy for heart attack risk. Drug X reduces BP via a pathway that doesn't reduce heart attacks. Right causal estimate, wrong outcome.
MISLEADING PROXY (The Outcome Problem)
Training program increases test scores (proxy for skill). But participants learn to pass tests, not to apply skills on the job. Campbell's Law: measured metric is gamed.
GOODHART'S LAW
'When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.' Police target arrest numbers → arrests increase but crime doesn't decrease. The metric is optimized, not the underlying goal.
4
Obligation of Means vs. Results
◆ GALLEA'S PHILOSOPHICAL ANCHOR
OBLIGATION OF MEANS
Use the best available methods and reasoning process. A doctor isn't liable for a patient dying if they followed best medical practice. Focus on process quality.
OBLIGATION OF RESULTS
Deliver the outcome regardless of method. A builder must deliver a functioning building. Outcome is everything; process is secondary.
THE CAUSAL THINKER'S STANCE: In a world of uncertainty, we operate under an obligation of means. Use the best causal reasoning available, acknowledge uncertainty explicitly, and make decisions on the best evidence — but don't pretend to certainty you don't have.

From Experiments to Equations

RCTs, Quasi-Experiments, and the Conquest of Mount Intervention
Gallea · Ch.6 & 7 Pearl · Ch.7
"We finally make our bold ascent onto the second level of the Ladder of Causation — the level of intervention." — Pearl
1
RCTs — Why Randomization Works
◆ PEARL'S FORMAL EXPLANATION OF THE GOLD STANDARD
Pearl explains what Gallea teaches: an RCT is the gold standard because randomization physically implements the do-operator — it severs all back-door paths by making treatment assignment independent of all confounders.
WHAT RANDOMIZATION DOES
Breaks the link between confounders and treatment. Both groups are statistically identical on average — observed and unobserved. The difference in outcomes IS the causal effect.
WHERE RCTS FAIL
Unethical (can't assign people to smoke). Expensive and slow. Non-compliance — people don't follow assignment. External validity — lab conditions differ from real world.
GALLEA'S 7 PRINCIPLES FOR RCTS: Random assignment, control group, blinding, intention-to-treat analysis, pre-registration, sufficient power, and transparent reporting. Each principle plugs a specific methodological hole.
2
Pearl's Three Deconfounding Routes
◆ WHEN RCTS ARE IMPOSSIBLE
Pearl provides three main routes to causal identification when experimental manipulation is not possible and some confounding variables are available. Gallea teaches the practical judgment for when to use each.
3
Quasi-Experimental Methods
◆ THREE METHODS FOR WHEN RCTS ARE IMPOSSIBLE
DIFFERENCE-IN-DIFFERENCES (DiD)
KEY ASSUMPTION: Parallel trends — treatment and control groups would have followed the same trajectory without intervention.
EXAMPLE: Countries that received arms vs. those that didn't. If both were trending similarly pre-treatment, the post-treatment divergence is the causal effect.
REGRESSION DISCONTINUITY (RDD)
KEY ASSUMPTION: Around a threshold, units just above and just below are essentially randomly assigned.
EXAMPLE: Spotify podcast promotion: shows just above/below a listener threshold. Those barely qualifying vs. barely missing are near-identical — making the threshold a 'natural experiment.'
INSTRUMENTAL VARIABLES (IV)
KEY ASSUMPTION: Exclusion restriction — the instrument affects outcome ONLY through treatment.
EXAMPLE: Price as instrument for demand: weather shocks affect supply (price), which affects quantity demanded. Weather has no direct effect on demand — only through price.
GALLEA'S KEY INSIGHT: Every quasi-experimental method makes an identifying assumption that cannot be tested from data alone. The causal mindset demands you state this assumption explicitly and defend it with domain knowledge — not hide it inside a regression.
4
The Front-Door Adjustment — Pearl's Surprise
◆ A METHOD INVISIBLE TO CLASSICAL STATISTICS
The front-door adjustment was unknown before causal diagrams. It solves the smoking-cancer problem: even without observing the hypothetical "smoking gene," we can estimate the causal effect by routing through tar deposits — the mediator.
STEP 1: X→M
Estimate P(tar | do(smoking)). The smoking gene's path to tar goes through Cancer (a collider) — already blocked. No confounding. Simple observation works.
STEP 2: M→Y
Estimate P(cancer | do(tar)). Back-door path exists via smoking gene. Block it by adjusting for smoking status. Standard back-door adjustment works.
COMBINE STEPS 1+2: → P(cancer | do(smoking)) — without ever measuring the smoking gene. This is what Pearl means by "mathematics does for us what ten years of debate could not."
5
The do-Calculus — Completeness
◆ IF A CAUSAL EFFECT CAN BE IDENTIFIED, THE DO-CALCULUS WILL FIND IT
Pearl's students proved that the do-calculus is complete. If there exists any way to identify a causal effect from a given diagram and dataset, the do-calculus will find it. If it can't, no method can — the effect is genuinely not identifiable from that data.
Rule 1: Ignoring obs.
P(Y|do(X),Z) = P(Y|do(X)) when Z is irrelevant given do(X)
Rule 2: Action/obs.
do(X) becomes observation when all back-door paths are blocked
Rule 3: Deletion
do(X) can be removed when X has no causal effect on Y

Counterfactuals & Mediation

Mining Worlds That Could Have Been — The Highest Rung
Pearl · Ch.8 & 9
"Counterfactuals are the building blocks of moral reasoning, scientific thought, and meaningful human communication." — Pearl
1
The SCM Algorithm — Three Steps to Any Counterfactual
◆ ABDUCTION → ACTION → PREDICTION
Pearl provides a mechanical procedure for answering any counterfactual question.
1. ABDUCTION
Use observed evidence to pin down the 'noise' — the individual's specific characteristics. Given the patient was untreated and died, what does this tell us about their susceptibility?
2. ACTION
Modify the model — set Treatment = 1, sever all incoming arrows to Treatment. This creates the counterfactual world.
3. PREDICTION
Run the modified model with the pinned noise. In this counterfactual world, does the patient survive? The answer is a probability.
THE CRUCIAL ASYMMETRY: Rung-2 questions are about populations. Rung-3 questions are about individuals who have already taken a specific action — you can never directly observe what would have happened otherwise. Only a model can answer them.
2
Necessity vs. Sufficiency — PN and PS
◆ THE LEGAL AND SCIENTIFIC LANGUAGE OF CAUSE
PN — PROBABILITY OF NECESSITY
"But for X, would Y have occurred?" The legal "but-for" test. If the defendant hadn't been negligent, would the harm still have happened? PN = 0 means the harm would have happened anyway (not liable under necessity standard).
PS — PROBABILITY OF SUFFICIENCY
"Was X sufficient to cause Y?" Even if other causes existed, was this one enough? PS = 1 means X alone would have caused Y. Many courts use a "substantial factor" test to handle redundant-cause cases.
THE FALLING PIANO — PEARL'S THOUGHT EXPERIMENT
A pedestrian is hit by both a car and a falling piano simultaneously — either alone would be fatal. PN = 0 (the piano would have killed them anyway — car not a "necessary" cause). PS = 1 (the car was sufficient to cause death). The right legal standard depends on which counterfactual question you're asking.
3
Climate Attribution — Counterfactuals in Practice
◆ FROM 'IMPOSSIBLE TO SAY' TO PRECISE PROBABILITY
OLD ANSWER
"You can never attribute a single weather event to climate change." Impossibility.
NEW ANSWER (SCM)
"90% probability that climate change was a necessary cause of this heat wave." Precise and defensible.
METHOD: Run two climate simulations — one with historical CO₂, one without. Probability of Necessity = P(heat wave | actual) − P(heat wave | counterfactual), normalized. This is counterfactual reasoning made operational at planetary scale.
4
Mediation — Knowing HOW, Not Just THAT
◆ LOOKING INSIDE THE BLACK BOX
RCTs tell us that a treatment works. Mediation analysis tells us how — through what mechanism. Aspirin prevents strokes, but is it through platelet aggregation or another path? The right policy depends on knowing the mechanism.
5
Berkeley Admissions — Where Everything Connects
◆ SIMPSON'S PARADOX + MEDIATION + COUNTERFACTUALS
UC Berkeley 1973: aggregate data shows bias against women. Stratified by department, the bias reverses. This is simultaneously Simpson's Paradox (Ch.6), a mediation story, and a counterfactual question. Department choice is a mediator between gender and admission.
DIRECT EFFECT (NDE)
Within each department, women were admitted equally or at higher rates. No direct discrimination.
INDIRECT EFFECT (NIE)
Women applied to more competitive departments. This is the indirect path: Gender → Department Choice → Admission.
THE POLICY QUESTION
Should Berkeley address why women choose harder departments (indirect path) or within-department bias (direct path)? Mediation analysis tells you which lever matters.

◆ WHEN CAN RESULTS FROM ONE STUDY APPLY ELSEWHERE?
Pearl's transportability theory answers: given a causal diagram showing differences between populations, can we still transfer the causal estimate?

Causal structure is identical in both populations. Results transfer directly.

Non-transportable

3
Causal Evidence Is a Spectrum
◆ FROM ANECDOTE TO CONVERGENT RCTS — NOT A BINARY
Weakest: Anecdote
'Took pill, had best sleep ever.' N=1, no comparison, could be coincidence, placebo, or a calmer evening.
Weak: Observational
People who take the pill sleep better — but self-selection: insomniacs take the pill AND have more motivation to improve sleep habits.
Moderate: Quasi-exp.
DiD, RDD, or IV study with explicit identifying assumption. Depends on assumption quality.
Strong: Single RCT
Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled. Internally valid but may not generalize.
Strongest: Multiple RCTs
Independent RCTs in different populations, contexts, and labs all pointing the same direction.
KEY REALIZATION: Most real-world decisions fall in the middle of this spectrum. Perfect evidence is rare. The causal mindset helps you assess where on the spectrum you are — and make the best decision given that position.
4
Healthy Skepticism vs. Destructive Nihilism
◆ THE RISK — AND THE ANSWER
BEFORE THESE BOOKS
Correlation feels like causation. Headlines taken at face value. "Studies show" is sufficient authority. Simple explanations go unchallenged.
AFTER THESE BOOKS
Every "because" triggers a question. Counterfactuals are instinctive. You assess evidence strength automatically. You challenge studies without dismissing science.
GALLEA'S DEFENSE
Teaching people to question evidence is like teaching people to read. The argument that it risks "anti-science skepticism" sounds like the medieval church preventing literacy to maintain authority. Scientific evidence remains the best tool we have. Scrutiny strengthens it.

Six Commitments for the Causal Thinker
◆ YOUR MANIFESTO — FROM BOTH AUTHORS
1. Question every "because"
Apply Gallea's 5-step framework instinctively. Every causal claim implies a counterfactual — challenge it.
2. Draw the diagram
Pearl's first commandment. Make your assumptions explicit as a DAG. The act of drawing reveals what you know and what you're guessing.
3. Calibrate your language

4. Distinguish prediction from causation
ML predicts beautifully. Causation requires a different toolkit. Don't confuse them — the stakes are too high.
5. Demand mechanism, not just effect
