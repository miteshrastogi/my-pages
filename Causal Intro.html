The Causal Mindset

A unified reading of The Book of Why (Pearl & Mackenzie, 2018) and The Causal Mindset Handbook (Gallea, Packt 2026) — arranged so the theory earns its weight through the practice, and the practice gains its rigor through the theory.

Pearl gives you the grammar: a ladder, a diagram, a calculus. Gallea gives you the habit: five steps to ask before trusting any claim, three questions to interrogate any correlation. Read either alone and you get half the picture — the mathematician's sketch, or the practitioner's checklist. Read them braided, and the sketch becomes the checklist's justification, while the checklist keeps the sketch honest to the messy worlds of policy, medicine, AI, and business decisions made in under an hour.

Each chapter below pairs one (sometimes two) chapters from each book on a shared question — what is causation?, why is it hard?, how do we measure it? — and ends with a bridge that motivates the next step. Navigate via the hamburger at top-left.

01FoundationsPart I

Why Causality Matters

The questions data refuses to answer — and the billion-dollar decisions we make anyway.
PEARL · PREFACE & INTRO GALLEA · PREFACE & CH 1
"You cannot answer a question that you cannot ask, and you cannot ask a question you have no words for." — Judea Pearl, The Book of Why, Introduction Pearl and Gallea open on the same conviction, phrased from opposite ends. Pearl writes as a scientist who watched statistics ban the word cause for a century. Gallea writes as an economist who watched executives stake fortunes on correlations, and mistake prediction for reason. Both arrive at the same place: most of the important questions we ask — in medicine, policy, business, our own lives — are causal, and for most of modern history we had no rigorous way to answer them.
1
Two diagnoses of the same gap

Read Pearl alone and you get the scientist's grievance: a formal language for cause was possible all along and science declined to build one. Read Gallea alone and you get the practitioner's grievance: decisions with enormous stakes are routinely made on evidence that would not pass an undergraduate research-methods course. The two complaints describe one hole in the edifice of modern knowledge, seen from two sides.

Same hole, two vantage points
Pearl · the theorist
Modern statistics was born from causal questions — Galton and Pearson asking why traits run in families — then, unable to answer them cleanly, declared the questions off-limits. For most of the 20th century, students were taught to say only that X and Y are "associated" or "correlated." Causal vocabulary was almost prohibited. "When you prohibit speech," Pearl writes, "you prohibit thought."
Gallea · the practitioner
Meanwhile, boardrooms and ministries kept making causal decisions anyway — just without the tools. "People were making big decisions based on opinions and correlations, and not causal evidence." Investments in AI of billions, driven by fear of missing out, with organizations unable to answer: is it actually working? The gap is not theoretical. It costs money, health, lives.
2
What it costs — four domains

Both books return, throughout, to a rotating cast of real cases. Four of them, drawn directly from Gallea and recognizable to any reader of Pearl, set the stakes:

POLICY · TRUMP & IMMIGRATIONTrump claimed his January 2021 immigration numbers were "the best ever" — lowest illegal crossings. But January 2021 was also mid-COVID, after a year of global lockdowns closed borders almost everywhere. Attribution matters: if you credit the policy when the pandemic did the work, the next administration inherits a false playbook.
HEALTH · ASPIRIN & THE HEADACHEPearl's recurring example: "Was it the aspirin that stopped my headache?" Until the 1990s, asking a statistician that question, Pearl writes, would have been "like asking if he believed in voodoo." Gallea uses the same example to introduce the fundamental problem: you can never observe yourself in two states at once.
SCIENCE · CHOCOLATE & NOBEL LAUREATESMesserli's 2012 NEJM paper found a strong country-level correlation between chocolate consumption and Nobel laureates per capita. Maurage & Pesenti rebutted it in 2013 with one move: GDP causes both. Classic fork. Both books use this case — Gallea as a working example in Ch. 4, Pearl in Ch. 2 as an illustration of what Pearson and Yule called spurious correlation a century before.
ENVIRONMENT · E-SCOOTERSAn e-scooter company brags its product is greener than cars. True — but the proxy counterfactual is wrong. Most e-scooter trips replace walking and public transit, which are greener still. Net effect: probably worse emissions. Pick the wrong comparison, reverse the sign of your conclusion.
3
Why we cannot just look harder at the data

A natural reaction to the stakes above is: we have so much data now. Surely enough data will sort this out. Both authors, writing from very different traditions, converge on a blunt answer. No. The problem is not data volume. The problem is on both sides of the lens — in the data, and in the mind holding the lens.

The two-sided limitation
Pearl · data are dumb
"Data are profoundly dumb." They can tell you that medicine-takers recovered faster than non-takers; they cannot tell you why. Maybe the takers could afford the medicine and the rest. The causal model is not in the data — it is brought to the data by the researcher, encoding assumptions about which variable listens to which.
Gallea · minds are biased
Meanwhile the human lens is warped. Confirmation bias finds evidence for what you already believe. Availability makes loud cases seem common. Apophenia sees patterns in pure noise. Gallea traces his own journey through the Dunning–Kruger curve — peak of enthusiasm, valley of despair, slope of enlightenment — and says this arc is a feature of learning to think causally.

The solution both authors end up proposing is the same: a disciplined pairing of domain knowledge (what the mind brings — structure, mechanism, assumptions) and data (what the world brings — observations, frequencies). Neither works without the other. Data plus a wrong model of the world is confident fiction; a model of the world without data is a just-so story.

4
The inference engine — and the mindset that uses it

Pearl sketches a blueprint he calls the causal inference engine. Three inputs — assumptions (encoded as a diagram), queries (written in a formal language we will meet in Chapter 6), data — produce three outputs: whether the query is answerable at all, a formula (estimand) if it is, and finally an estimate with uncertainty. The engine is mechanical. What it needs is someone who knows the domain well enough to draw the diagram.

Gallea does not redraw this engine; he operationalizes it. His entire five-step Causal Mindset framework is a field-grade procedure for producing its first input — the assumptions — when you have ten minutes before a meeting, not ten weeks for a paper. We will meet all five steps in Chapter 6. What matters now is that the theorist's blueprint and the practitioner's checklist are the same artifact, seen at two zoom levels.

If I could sum up the message of this book in one pithy phrase, it would be that you are smarter than your data. Data do not understand causes and effects; humans do. — Pearl, closing the Introduction
Bridge to Chapter 2
Before we can use the engine, we need to know what kinds of questions it answers — and what kinds lie beyond it. Pearl's answer is a ladder with three rungs: seeing, doing, imagining. Every question in the opening of this chapter belongs to one of those rungs. So does every tool, including all the tools Gallea will teach us. Chapter 2 climbs it.

02FoundationsPart I

The Ladder of Causation

Seeing · Doing · Imagining — and why modern AI is still on the first rung.
PEARL · CH 1 GALLEA · CH 1 & CH 2
"A causal learner must master at least three distinct levels of cognitive ability: seeing, doing, and imagining." — Pearl, The Book of Why, Ch. 1 This is the central organizing metaphor of Pearl's book, and — though Gallea never draws it as a ladder — it is also the implicit scaffolding of the Causal Mindset Handbook. Every tool either lives on a rung, or moves us from a lower rung to a higher one.
1
The three rungs

Pearl arranges causal reasoning into three tiers, each strictly more powerful than the one below. A system on a lower rung cannot answer the queries of a higher one, no matter how much data it ingests. Tap a rung.

RUNG 3 · IMAGINING · COUNTERFACTUALS
"What if I had acted differently?"
P(YX=x | X=x′, Y=y′)probability of Y in a hypothetical where X had been x, given we actually observed X=x′, Y=y′
Retrospective, imaginative, uniquely human. Required for questions of regret, blame, responsibility, explanation, and mechanism. The law needs this rung (did the defendant's action cause the harm?). So does medicine (would the patient have died without treatment?). Pearl argues this is what separates human cognition from animal cognition — and from current AI.
RUNG 2 · DOING · INTERVENTION
"What will happen if I do X?"
P(Y | do(X))probability of Y when we set X by intervention, not observe it
Seeing smoke is different from making smoke. Pearl's running example: "What happens to floss sales if we double the price of toothpaste?" Cannot be answered from sales logs alone, because in past observations the price changed for a reason (supply shock, competitor move). You need either an experiment, or a model strong enough to simulate one. RCTs live here. So does every A/B test.
RUNG 1 · SEEING · ASSOCIATION
"How are X and Y related?"
P(Y | X)conditional probability of Y, given we observe X
Pattern-matching. An owl hunting a rat. Correlations, regressions, classification. Every present-day machine-learning system lives here — including deep learning, despite its sophistication. It fits functions to observations. It does not ask what would change if the environment changed.
2
One decision, three rungs — Pearl's toothpaste

Pearl illustrates all three rungs through a single grocery-store example — the kind a sales manager actually faces. Watch the question change shape as we climb:

RUNG 1
"How likely is a customer who bought toothpaste to also buy floss?" — Purely observational. Count them in the data. No causal claim — could go either way, or both be driven by hygiene-conscious customers.
RUNG 2
"What will happen to floss sales if we double the price of toothpaste?" — Now we are intervening on the store. Past data, where prices moved for reasons, will not answer this cleanly. Need to run the experiment, or model the market.
RUNG 3
"What is the probability that a customer who bought toothpaste would still have bought it had we doubled the price?" — This compares the actual world to a fictitious world. Data from the actual world alone cannot answer it. Requires a causal model of the customer's decision.
3
Why deep learning sits on rung 1

This is Pearl's most contentious claim in the book, and the one Gallea echoes loudly in his Chapter 2. Modern machine learning, however impressive, operates "almost entirely in an associational mode." It fits a function to a stream of observations, the way a statistician fits a line. Add more layers, add more data, you get a more accurate function — not a smarter one.

The driverless car test
Pearl's thought experiment: a driverless car trained on millions of miles of data will not, on its own, figure out that a pedestrian holding a whiskey bottle may respond differently to a honking horn. The programmers must add that. A rung-1 system has no model of the environment to do counterfactual reasoning with. It only has the function it has fit.
4.6 folded Pearl's ML critique into the opening chapter as background. In v2, we let the ladder do the work here — then use it to motivate a full dedicated chapter (Ch 3) on Gallea's prediction-vs-causation argument, where the stakes are drawn out concretely for AI products in the wild.
4
Where Gallea's toolkit sits

Gallea never draws the ladder, but his book is a guided tour up it. Here is the map, which will be useful to keep in mind for the rest of this reader:

RUNG 1 — WHAT GALLEA TEACHES YOU TO DISTRUSTAny causal claim backed only by correlation. Ch. 1 teaches the DAG. Ch. 4 teaches the three culprits. These are the alarm system for rung-1 arguments masquerading as rung-2 or 3.
RUNG 2 — HOW GALLEA TEACHES YOU TO GET THEREThe five-step Mindset (Ch. 5), randomized experiments (Ch. 6), and quasi-experimental methods — DiD, IV, RD, synthetic control (Ch. 7). Each one is a way to approximate do(X) without the experiment, or with it.
RUNG 3 — WHERE GALLEA STAYS HUMBLECh. 8 (correct answer, wrong question) and Ch. 9 (embracing uncertainty) sit here. Even after you climb, the top rung keeps reminding you that your causal effect is an answer to your chosen question — and other questions may matter more.
Bridge to Chapter 3
One rung of the ladder deserves a full chapter of its own — rung 1, because it is the rung almost everyone starts from, and where modern AI is stuck. Gallea dedicates Chapter 2 of the Handbook to the exact shape of the error it makes. It is a chapter almost every data professional should read twice. We give it its own space next.

03FoundationsPart I

Prediction ≠ Causation

Why "if all you have is a hammer" is not just a saying in the ML era.
GALLEA · CH 2 (PRIMARY) PEARL · INTRO & CH 1 (ECHO)
"If all you have is a hammer, everything looks like a nail." — the law of the instrument, Abraham Maslow — Gallea, The Causal Mindset Handbook, opening of Ch. 2 Pearl devotes a few paragraphs of his Introduction and Ch. 1 to the ML-vs-causality distinction. Gallea devotes a whole chapter — and with good reason. The tools people actually reach for to answer causal questions today are predictive ML models. The confusion runs deeper than statistics 101. This chapter, new in v2, draws it out.
4.6 did not give this topic its own chapter; it absorbed Gallea Ch. 2 into the Ch. 1 framing. That was a missed opportunity — this is the most practically urgent chapter of Gallea's book for anyone working adjacent to AI. v2 gives it full weight.
1
The thin terminology frontier

Gallea opens with a simple rule that, if followed, would eliminate an astonishing fraction of published nonsense. For non-causal relationships, say "associated with" or "correlated with." For causal ones, say "impacts" or "increases." Never cross the line by accident. But most writers do.

A CNN headline walks into a fallacy
The article: "6 exercises that address an often overlooked key to longevity." The body reports a strong correlation between grip strength and longevity. Scientific — fine. But the article then recommends exercises to increase grip strength, with the implication that doing so will extend your life.
Gallea's move: The underlying paper (López-Bueno et al., 2022) uses the precise word "association." The journalist correctly quotes "associated with." Then, in the same breath, recommends an intervention. The causal leap is unearned.
The likely truth: A common cause — overall physical activity — drives both grip strength and longevity. Training your grip in isolation may not help you live longer. Gallea's Ch. 4 will give this common-cause structure a name: confounder.

The lesson scales. Every time you read "associated with" followed by an action recommendation, someone has quietly crossed from rung 1 to rung 2 without paying the fare. The fare is causal evidence.

2
When to use which

Gallea is not anti-ML. He is anti-confusion. Here is his decision matrix, distilled — which tool fits which question?

GOAL: FORECAST, FLAG, RANK
Use predictive ML when the question is what is likely to happen / who is likely to do what, the environment is stable, and you do not need to explain why.
  • Customer churn probability — given historical patterns
  • Fraud flagging — detect anomalies in transactions
  • Recommendation ranking — what this user will click
  • Image classification, translation, speech-to-text
Danger: if the environment changes (regulation, new competitor, different market), a predictive model trained on the old world can fail silently. It has no causal structure to reason from.
GOAL: DECIDE, INTERVENE, ATTRIBUTE
Use causal inference when the question is what will happen if we change something, or did our action cause this outcome.
  • Did the marketing campaign cause the sales lift?
  • Will this new pricing strategy keep more customers?
  • What will happen to emissions if we ban combustion engines?
  • Was the AI product actually responsible for the productivity gain?
Gallea's observation: These are the questions organizations most need to answer — and most frequently try to answer with predictive models, because those are what the team already has.
3
The productive collaboration

Gallea's deepest point in Ch. 2 is not "ML bad, causal good." It is that the two tools are complementary, and the best operators use both. Causal inference can answer the question did our ML model add value? — a question the ML model cannot answer about itself.

A three-step hybrid Gallea sees in the wild
STEP 1 · PREDICT
Build a predictive model (traditional ML): who is likely to churn, which transactions look fraudulent, which ad creative will rank highest.
STEP 2 · DEPLOY
Roll the model into production — ideally via randomized rollout or a phased deployment that creates a comparison.
STEP 3 · ATTRIBUTE
Use causal inference to measure the impact of the deployed model on the metric that matters. This is what Pearl would call moving from rung 1 to rung 2 with a specific tool.

Gallea notes that the emerging field of Causal AI (and libraries like DoWhy, EconML, and the newer pywhyllm) is precisely this pattern baked into the modeling stack. Pearl would call it the inference engine with ML plugged in where estimation happens.

Bridge to Chapter 4
We now know why we want to climb the ladder, and which tool suits which rung. Part II of this reader opens a different question: why is rung 2 so hard to reach? Pearl tells us one reason — a century of statistical history that outlawed the attempt. Gallea tells us another — a fundamental problem of comparison that no amount of cleverness can fully escape. Both stories converge on the concept we will live with for the rest of the book: the proxy counterfactual.

04The ChallengePart II

The Lost Century

How statistics outlawed the word cause — and why, even with the word restored, causal proof remains structurally impossible.
PEARL · CH 2 GALLEA · CH 3
"When you prohibit speech, you prohibit thought and stifle principles, methods, and tools." — Pearl, The Book of Why, Introduction Pearl's Ch. 2 is a history of a self-inflicted wound; Gallea's Ch. 3 is a diagnosis of the wound underneath the wound. Together they explain why, even after statistics rediscovered causality, the core challenge remains — and what concept now carries all the weight: the proxy counterfactual.
1
Pearl's history of a self-inflicted wound

Modern statistics was born, ironically, from a causal question. Francis Galton (Darwin's cousin) wanted to know whether tall fathers produced tall sons — a question about inheritance as a cause. His pupil Karl Pearson inherited the data and the problem. Both discovered the correlation coefficient, both could measure association crisply, and neither could untangle it into cause. Rather than diagnose the limit of their tools, they declared the question unscientific. In his Grammar of Science (1892), Pearson dismissed causation as "a fetish amidst the inscrutable arcana of modern science" — a phrase Pearl quotes directly in Ch. 2 to characterize the century-long hostility that followed.

The one hero in Pearl's telling: Sewall Wright. A geneticist, not a statistician, Wright invented path diagrams in the 1920s — dots-and-arrows pictures of causal structure, with coefficients attached — to answer questions Pearson would not let statisticians ask. He did it essentially alone, was ignored or dismissed for decades, and his method lay embryonic until Pearl and others rediscovered it in the 1980s. Every DAG Gallea asks you to draw descends from Wright's 1921 paper Correlation and Causation, which asked how much a guinea pig's birth weight changes if it spends one more day in the womb — and showed, via the path diagram, that the naive answer (5.66 g/day) was biased by litter size.

Statistics fetishized "correlation is not causation." It told us what causation isn't, but not what it is. In vain will you search the index of a statistics textbook for an entry on "cause." — Pearl, The Book of Why, Introduction
2
Gallea's fundamental problem — parallel worlds

Suppose statistics had never gone silent. Suppose the word cause had never been banned. We would still run into the wall Gallea describes in Ch. 3 — the wall causal inference calls its fundamental problem. The scenario is almost insultingly simple, and so is the problem:

The headache & the pill
Your head aches. You hold out, drink tea, eventually take the pill. Later — better. Did the pill cause it? You cannot know. To know, with certainty, you would need two copies of you and the universe — identical from the Big Bang to the headache — one that takes the pill, one that does not. Any difference in the two futures is then attributable to the one difference in their histories. This is the only way to measure a causal effect with perfect certainty. And it is impossible.

Gallea gives this concept a name you will use for the rest of the reader: the key. Whenever you encounter a causal claim, the key unlocks the question — what is being compared here, and how far is that comparison from the ideal of parallel worlds?

The impossibility is not philosophical hand-waving. It is a precise constraint on measurement. You cannot observe the same unit — person, country, lab rat, company — in two states at once. Pick any claim you have ever encountered; the ideal test for it requires two versions of a world that cannot coexist.

3
The proxy counterfactual — the concept everything else rests on

If the ideal is impossible, we substitute. Gallea calls this substitute the proxy counterfactual — a real-world stand-in for the unobservable parallel world. Every causal method in the rest of this book, from randomized trials to regression discontinuity to Pearl's do-calculus, is ultimately in the business of finding a good proxy. The question that separates rigorous work from speculation is how close does your proxy get to the ideal?

PARALLEL-GROUP DESIGN — STANDARD RCT
Lab mice from inbred strains — bred for over 20 generations of sibling matings, so nearly genetically identical — are randomly split. One group gets the drug, one the placebo. Identical food, cages, handling. Any difference at the end is attributable to the drug. Gallea's caveat: almost identical. Even tiny systematic differences (one cage nearer the door = louder = stressed) leak into the comparison. The closer to parallel, the stronger the claim.
CROSSOVER / SWITCHBACK DESIGN
Same animals (or Uber drivers, or users) get treatment on day A and placebo on day B. The unit is its own control — a closer approximation to parallel worlds than two different groups. Danger: order effects, experimenter drift, unobserved changes between the two days. Bojinov, Simchi-Levi & Zhao's work on "switchback experiments" formalizes this for platforms like Uber.
WITHIN-UNIT · SAME-SUBJECT DESIGN
Treatment on one eye, placebo on the other. Treatment on the left paw, placebo on the right. The same individual, at the same time, in different places. This gets closest to the parallel-worlds ideal — but it works only for outcomes that stay local to the treated site. Systemic effects break the design.
OBSERVATIONAL · WHAT'S LEFT WHEN YOU CAN'T EXPERIMENT
Most questions — climate policy, mass education, the macro economy — cannot be randomized. You take what the world gave you and you look for structure that approximates a parallel world: a policy change, a threshold, an instrument, a synthetic control. The entire quasi-experimental toolbox in Ch. 8 is built for this case.
Bridge to Chapter 5
You now have the key: every causal claim has a hidden comparison, and the proxy counterfactual is how close that comparison gets to the ideal. But the ideal has internal structure — there are specific ways a proxy can fail, and specific diagram shapes that produce each failure. Pearl gives us the grammar. Gallea gives us the field guide. Chapter 5 merges them.

05The ChallengePart II

The Grammar of Cause

DAGs, three junction types, and Gallea's three culprits — a unified field guide to what can go wrong.
PEARL · CH 3 GALLEA · CH 4
"Bayesian networks were my personal route into causality — and ultimately the tool I had to transcend." — Pearl, The Book of Why, Ch. 3 Pearl arrives at causal DAGs by way of Bayes's rule and Bayesian networks — the tools that got him to think in "shades of gray," before he realized they were not enough. Gallea starts from the other end: the practitioner looking at a news article and asking, what specifically could be wrong with this claim? They meet in the middle, at the same three structural shapes.
1
Every causal diagram reduces to three junctions

Pearl's central technical move in Ch. 3: any DAG, no matter how elaborate, is built from exactly three types of three-node junctions. Learn the three, and you can read any diagram. Tap each:

B IS A MEDIATOR
A causes C through B. Example: exercise → heart health → longevity. The effect of A on C is real; B is the pathway. When you control for B, you block the effect. Do not "control for" a mediator if you want the total effect of A on C. Gallea's Ch. 4 uses the roadblock-by-activists example: roadblock → environmental awareness → pollution reduction. Awareness is the mediator.
B IS A CONFOUNDER
B causes both A and C. There is no causal arrow between A and C — but they will be correlated, because B moves them together. Chocolate → Nobel laureates has no causal arrow; GDP is the fork. Ice cream sales → shark attacks: sunny weather is the fork. This is the single most common failure of causal reasoning in the wild. Gallea's first diagnostic question — is there something else? — is a fork-detector.
B IS A COLLIDER
Both A and C cause B. By default, A and C are not associated. But control for B — and you induce a spurious association. Gallea's example: diabetes and lung disease are unrelated in the population, but both raise hospitalization rates. If you study only hospitalized patients, the two conditions appear correlated. This is Berkson's paradox. Key rule: never control for a collider. It is the opposite of a confounder.

Pearl's gift is that these three exhaust the possibilities. Whatever else you draw, it is built from these three pieces. Gallea's gift is that each has a field-ready name: mediator, confounder, collider. Memorize the three. They do most of the work.

2
Gallea's three culprits — the field guide

Pearl gives you the grammar; Gallea gives you the inspector's checklist. Ch. 4 of the Handbook is organized around three diagnostic questions, each aimed at a specific failure mode. Two map cleanly onto Pearl's junctions. The third — external validity — is genuinely new, and arguably underweighted by theorists:

CULPRIT 1
"Is there something else?" — Confounders. The fork, in Pearl's grammar. Technically: omitted variable bias or a back-door path. A third variable moves both the cause and the outcome. Class size → academic performance looks strong — until you notice socio-economic status drives both. Always the first question to ask.
CULPRIT 2
"Could it be the reverse?" — Reverse causation. Police → crime: does more police cause less crime, or does more crime attract more police? Often the arrow points the other way. In a DAG, this is just a question about which direction the arrows point — but in prose, it hides easily.
CULPRIT 3
"Can we extrapolate?" — External validity. Even a clean causal effect, perfectly measured, may not generalize. A drug trial on 60-year-old men may not apply to 30-year-old women. A policy that worked in Denmark may fail in Nigeria. Pearl calls the formal version of this problem transportability (Ch. 10). Gallea bakes it into his five-step check from the start.
4.6 mentioned Gallea's three questions but did not map them to Pearl's junctions or flag external validity as a distinctive contribution. v2 surfaces both — because this is exactly where Gallea's practice work adds something Pearl's theory underweights.
3
What to control, what not to control

A practical consequence both books hammer: controlling for a variable — in a regression, in an RCT stratification, in a causal DAG — is not always good. It depends on the junction. Here is the lookup table:

CONFOUNDER (FORK)
Do control for it. Closes the back-door path. Restores a clean causal estimate.
MEDIATOR (CHAIN)
Depends on the question. Blocks total effect; reveals direct effect. See Ch. 9.
COLLIDER
Do NOT control for it. Opens a spurious path. Creates associations out of thin air.

This is why Gallea warns, explicitly in Ch. 2, that automated feature selection — the bread and butter of predictive ML — is dangerous for causal work. An algorithm throws out "unpredictive" confounders (keeping them changes nothing for prediction, but they're vital for causation) and keeps "predictive" colliders (helpful for prediction, ruinous for causal inference). Causal modeling depends on domain knowledge of the structure. No algorithm substitutes for it.

06The ToolkitPart III

Confounding, the Mindset & the Smoke-Filled Room

Pearl's do-operator meets Gallea's five steps — stress-tested against the smoking-cancer debate that almost killed epidemiology.
PEARL · CH 4 & CH 5 GALLEA · CH 5
"Confounding is not a statistical notion. It stands for the discrepancy between what we want to assess and what we actually do assess using statistical methods." — Pearl, The Book of Why, Ch. 4 4.6 split these into separate chapters — "Confounders & Bias" and "Causal Mindset in Action." v2 fuses them, because the 5-step framework is how a practitioner implements the do-operator in the field, and the smoking case is the proof-of-concept for the whole unified machinery.
1
The do-operator — confounding made precise

Pearl's great formal contribution in Ch. 4 is to define confounding — finally, precisely, and without appeal to intuition. For a century, statisticians used "confounder" as a loose heuristic. Pearl gives it one clean definition, using the do-operator we met in Ch. 2:

X and Y are confounded ⟺ P(Y | X) ≠ P(Y | do(X))The observational distribution differs from the interventional one. Seeing is not doing.

Two readings of the same formula. First: confounding is the gap between what the data show and what an experiment would show. Second: confounding is present whenever there exists an open back-door path from X to Y — a non-causal route through which the two variables can influence each other. The back-door criterion, also in Ch. 4, tells you exactly which variables to control for to close all back-door paths, recovering the interventional distribution from observational data alone.

That last sentence is a quiet miracle. It means that, given the right diagram, you can answer rung-2 questions without running the experiment — using only observational data plus your assumptions about structure. This is the hinge of the Causal Revolution. Everything in Ch. 8 (RCTs, IV, DiD, RD, synthetic control, do-calculus) is a different tactic for closing the back-door.

2
The Causal Mindset — five steps in a meeting

Gallea's Ch. 5 is the book's spine. He calls it the Causal Mindset framework: a five-step procedure short enough to run in your head while a colleague presents a claim. Each step is a question. Answering any one of them negatively can kill a weak argument before it spreads.

STEP 1 · WHAT IS THE CONTROL GROUP OR PROXY COUNTERFACTUAL?
Identify the comparison being made. How close is it to parallel worlds? Gallea notes this single step kills most weak claims. The Tesla autopilot article comparing raw crash counts to other makers? Fatal flaw at step 1 — no adjustment for fleet size. The e-scooter emissions claim? Fatal — wrong counterfactual (it replaces walking, not driving).
STEP 2 · IS THERE SOMETHING ELSE? (CONFOUNDERS / REVERSE CAUSATION)
Once the comparison survives step 1, ask: is there a third variable driving both X and Y? Could the arrow run the other way? The chocolate-Nobel case dies here (GDP confounds). The police-crime case dies here (reverse causation). In Pearl's language: any open back-door path?
STEP 3 · WHAT IS THE DIRECTION OF BIAS?
Even when you cannot fully measure a confounder, you can often sign its effect. Is the reported effect probably inflated or attenuated by the bias? If a study claims a small positive effect and the bias is plausibly in the same direction as the effect, the true effect may be near zero. If the bias is in the opposite direction, the true effect is probably even larger than reported. This alone often saves a marginal study — or kills one.
STEP 4 · REFINE & ITERATE
Propose a better comparison. Find a sharper subgroup. Adjust for the confounder you identified in step 2. This is where the Mindset either lets you rescue the analysis or declare it unsalvageable. Gallea's own research on arms transfers and conflict in Africa went through many refinements of this kind before publication.
STEP 5 · CAN WE EXTRAPOLATE?
Even if the effect is real here, will it hold there? The drug works for this population — will it work for yours? The policy worked in 2019 — will it work post-pandemic? External validity is separate from internal validity and requires its own argument. Pearl's Ch. 10 picks this up as transportability.
The causal mindset is about transforming you from a passive consumer of information into an active detective. — Gallea, Causal Mindset Handbook, Ch. 5
3
The test case — does smoking cause lung cancer?

Pearl devotes Ch. 5 to this one battle — and rightly. It is, he argues, the moment the new causal language proved it could settle a question that the old statistics genuinely could not. And the stakes were not abstract. Millions of lives.

The antagonists
FISHER'S OBJECTION
Ronald Fisher — statistical genius, lifetime pipe smoker, and later recipient of tobacco-industry consulting fees — argued that the smoking-cancer correlation could be explained by a shared genetic cause. Some gene, perhaps, disposes people to both smoke and to get lung cancer. In Pearl's grammar: an unobserved fork. Pearl's judgment on Fisher, to be fair, is that "it is very unlikely that tobacco money corrupted him in any way. His own obstinacy was sufficient." Fisher was not crazy — he was being consistent with rung-1 reasoning, which could not disprove the confounder hypothesis from association data alone.
CORNFIELD'S INEQUALITY
Jerome Cornfield (1959) made the killer move. He calculated: for an unobserved genetic confounder to explain away the observed smoking-cancer association, that gene would have to be nine times more common in smokers than in non-smokers. No such gene has ever been found. The hypothetical confounder would have to be implausibly huge.
THE VERDICT · 1964
The U.S. Surgeon General's 1964 report stated in no uncertain terms: "Cigarette smoking is causally related to lung cancer in men." The evidence was not a randomized controlled trial (impossible, unethical). It was a web of converging observational studies, dose-response relationships, animal experiments (rats painted with cigarette tar developed cancer), and — critically — Cornfield's sensitivity argument. Rung-2 reasoning built from rung-1 data, using tools Pearl would later formalize.
POSTSCRIPT · 2008
Fisher was partially vindicated, in a very limited way. In 2008, researchers identified rs16969968 — a gene on chromosome 15 that codes for nicotine receptors — dubbed "Mr. Big." People with two copies of the risky variant have roughly a 77% greater lung-cancer risk. So a smoking gene does exist. But Pearl's point stands: smokers have a ten-fold risk increase, and no gene can explain that magnitude. The gene is a mediator-partner, not a confounder that erases the causal story.

Pearl's argument is that the debate stretched to two decades precisely because statistics had no language for the question. Once Pearl's framework is in place, the debate takes minutes: draw the diagram, apply the back-door criterion, bound the possible confounder, done. Gallea's Mindset does the same work in five steps — and the smoking debate is, on retrospective inspection, an exhaustive application of those five steps over 20 years of arguments.

Bridge to Chapter 7

Pearl devotes Ch. 6 to the classical paradoxes — Simpson's, Berkson's, Monty Hall — where causal intuition wins over statistical intuition. Gallea devotes Ch. 8 to what he calls "correct answer, wrong question" (a Type III error) and the Goodhart trap. The two chapters describe one deeper theme: the metric you choose is a causal decision. Next.

Paradoxes & the Right Question

Simpson. Berkson. Monty Hall. Goodhart. Type III errors. What the famous puzzles teach us about choosing a question.
PEARL · CH 6 GALLEA · CH 8
"He who confronts the paradoxical exposes himself to reality." — epigraph to Pearl, Ch. 6 4.6 treated Pearl's paradoxes and Gallea's "correct answer, wrong question" as separate topics in separate chapters. The deeper reading fuses them: every classical paradox is a signal that we asked the wrong question, and Gallea's Ch. 8 generalizes the lesson. v2 runs them together.
1
Three paradoxes, one cause

Pearl's thesis is that the famous statistical paradoxes are not about probability at all. They are about causal structure — and they feel paradoxical only because our intuition is running the causal program while the statistics textbook is running the associational one. The paradoxes are, in Pearl's lovely phrase, "canaries in the coal mine" warning us that human intuition was right all along.

SIMPSON'S PARADOX
A drug helps men. It helps women. It hurts people overall. How? A confounder — e.g., sicker patients self-select into treatment — reverses the apparent sign when groups are aggregated. The resolution is causal, not statistical: the question "does the drug work?" has different answers depending on which population you plan to treat, and the choice is structural. Pearl's point: statisticians arguing about the paradox for half a century were arguing about which conditioning set to use — which is a causal decision disguised as a statistical one.
BERKSON'S PARADOX
Among hospitalized patients, two independent diseases appear negatively correlated — as though having one protects against the other. They do not. Both raise hospitalization (a collider). Conditioning on hospitalization opens a non-causal path between them. This is the collider rule from Ch. 5 biting in the wild. Same mechanism: celebrities with equal talent and looks selected into fame because either trait alone suffices — so talent and looks appear anti-correlated among celebrities.
MONTY HALL
Three doors; a car behind one. You pick. Monty opens a different door, revealing a goat. Switch? Yes — your odds jump from 1/3 to 2/3. The paradox melts the moment you notice Monty's choice is causally informed: his decision depends on where the car is. His opening a door is not a random event; it is intervention with inside knowledge. A collider-style argument explains it cleanly.

Pearl's deeper point: every one of these paradoxes dissolves when drawn as a diagram and read with the rules of Chapter 5. They are not paradoxes about probability. They are paradoxes about what you are allowed to condition on, and the answer is always given by the structure.

2
Correct answer, wrong question — Type III errors

Gallea's Ch. 8 takes the paradox lesson and scales it. Even if your methods are clean, your comparison honest, your diagram correct — you can still be deeply wrong, if the question was wrong. Statisticians have a name for this: a Type III error. "Correctly answering the wrong question."

Where Type III errors come from
WRONG OUTCOMEMeasuring reach when you care about persuasion. Counting downloads when you care about active users. Optimizing click-through when you care about long-term revenue.
WRONG POPULATIONA clean RCT result generalized to a population it was never tested on. The top of Ch. 5's external-validity question, now biting at the decision level.
WRONG TIME HORIZONShort-run effect of a price cut (great) vs long-run effect (margin erosion). Causal inference answers whichever horizon you modeled.
WRONG TREATMENT DEFINITION"Did the chatbot cause the productivity lift?" — but what counts as using the chatbot? Five uses? Daily use? The answer changes the effect.
When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. — Goodhart's Law, as cited by Gallea

Gallea pulls Goodhart's Law into the Type III discussion deliberately. Every metric you optimize becomes a target. Every target changes incentives. Every incentive shift changes the causal structure of what you are measuring. Teachers paid on standardized scores teach to the test. Salespeople paid on quarterly revenue smooth deals across quarters. The causal structure reorganizes around the metric. What you measured last year no longer measures what you thought.

Bridge to Chapter 8
We have the grammar (Ch. 5), the procedure (Ch. 6), and the humility to pick our questions carefully (Ch. 7). Now we need the actual machinery — the methods for turning rung-1 data into rung-2 answers. Pearl gives us the formal apparatus: do-calculus, back-door, front-door, instrumental variables. Gallea gives us the four families practitioners actually use: RCTs, diff-in-diff, regression discontinuity, synthetic control. Chapter 8 is where the ladder finally becomes a staircase.

Interventions — RCTs, Quasi-Experiments & do-Calculus

The methods that actually close the back-door. When each earns its keep.
PEARL · CH 7 GALLEA · CH 6 & CH 7
"He whose actions exceed his theory, his theory shall not last." — epigraph to Pearl, Ch. 7 This is the workhorse chapter of the reader. Pearl's Ch. 7 ("Conquest of Mount Intervention") is where do-calculus, the back-door and front-door criteria, and instrumental variables are introduced. Gallea's Ch. 6 and Ch. 7 are the practitioner's versions of exactly the same tools — with code, examples, and a clear-eyed accounting of when each fails.
1
The randomized experiment — the gold standard, and its limits

An RCT — randomized controlled trial — is Gallea's Ch. 6 in one sentence: randomize the treatment, and by construction you sever every back-door path. From Pearl's perspective this is magic: randomization mathematically equates P(Y|X) with P(Y|do(X)), because the treatment is now independent of all possible confounders by design. The RCT is not special because it uses data. It is special because it uses intervention.

What a real RCT requires — and why most "A/B tests" aren't quite RCTs
RANDOMIZATION
Genuinely random assignment. Not alphabetical. Not "first hundred users who sign up." Not "volunteers." Violations break the back-door guarantee.
COMPLIANCE
People assigned to treatment must actually get it. In medicine this is hard (drop-outs). In software A/B tests this is easier (the server decides), which is why A/B testing works better than most medical trials.
NO SPILLOVER
One user's treatment must not affect another's outcome. Vaccinations break this (herd immunity). Marketplaces break this (one user's new price affects another's choice set). Gallea notes this is the single most common failure mode of tech A/B tests.
ETHICS & FEASIBILITY
You cannot randomly assign smoking. You cannot randomly assign minimum wages to cities. Most policy questions and many health questions are unrandomizable — which is why quasi-experiments exist.
2
The quasi-experimental menu — four weapons

When you cannot randomize, you look for structure in the world that mimics randomization. Gallea's Ch. 7 is a tour of four families. Each one is a different way to close the back-door using observed features. Tap each for its mechanism and a concrete use-case:

DIFFERENCES-IN-DIFFERENCES · DID
Compare before-vs-after change in the treated group against before-vs-after change in an untreated comparison group. The subtraction kills any fixed difference between groups and any shared trend. Gallea's own application: the Swiss energy sobriety campaign of autumn 2022, where Switzerland is the treated unit and a carefully chosen comparison neighborhood provides the control trajectory. The canonical textbook example — Card & Krueger's 1994 New Jersey minimum-wage study — comes from Angrist & Pischke's Mostly Harmless Econometrics, which Gallea cites but does not rehash in detail.
REGRESSION DISCONTINUITY · RD
A bureaucratic threshold assigns treatment. Scholarship at 80% score — no scholarship at 79.9%. Units just below vs just above the cutoff are nearly identical in every way except treatment. The jump in outcomes at the cutoff is the causal effect. Works whenever a continuous score creates a sharp rule. Used extensively in education (class-size effects), banking (loan approvals), public health (age-based benefit eligibility).
INSTRUMENTAL VARIABLE · IV
Find an external instrument that influences the treatment X but does not affect the outcome Y through any other path. Pearl's historical example (Ch. 7): Sewall Wright's father Philip Wright used yield per acre of flaxseed as an instrument in 1928 — the earliest deliberate IV application outside Wright Jr.'s own work. Pearl's modern example: the 1984 Coronary Primary Prevention Trial for cholestyramine, where random treatment assignment served as an instrument for actual compliance. The canonical textbook example — Angrist's 1990 Vietnam draft-lottery study of lifetime earnings — is in Angrist & Pischke, which Gallea cites.
SYNTHETIC CONTROL · SCM
When there is only one treated unit (a single country passes a law; a single city launches a campaign), you cannot form a simple control group. SCM builds a synthetic version of the treated unit — a weighted combination of untreated donor units whose pre-treatment trajectory mimics the treated one's. The post-treatment gap between real and synthetic is the causal effect. The canonical textbook example is Abadie, Diamond & Hainmueller's application to California's Proposition 99 tobacco-control law; Gallea covers the method at a conceptual level in Ch. 7 without citing Prop 99 specifically. Now standard in policy evaluation where randomization is impossible and only one unit is treated.
3
Pearl's unifying machine — do-calculus

What Pearl offers in Ch. 7 is the deep structure underneath all four quasi-experimental methods: a calculus of interventions. Given a DAG and a query P(Y | do(X)), three rewriting rules (the rules of do-calculus) tell you whether the query is answerable from observational data, and if so, how. The rules are:

RULE 1
Permits the addition or deletion of observations — when a variable is irrelevant to the outcome given what else you know, you can add or drop it from the conditioning set. Pearl's example: Fire is irrelevant to Alarm once you already know Smoke.
RULE 2
Permits the replacement of an intervention with an observation, or vice versa — when a set Z satisfies the back-door criterion, do(X) and see(X) become equivalent conditional on Z. This is the rule that powers back-door adjustment.
RULE 3
Permits the deletion or addition of interventions — if there is no causal path from X to Y (no forward-arrow route), then intervening on X does not change Y's distribution. P(Y | do(X)) = P(Y).

Don't memorize the rules. Do absorb the punchline: in the mid-2000s, Pearl's student Ilya Shpitser — building on Jin Tian's c-decomposition method — developed a complete algorithm for do-calculus. "Complete" in the strict sense: if any sequence of these rules can reduce your interventional query to an observational formula, Shpitser's algorithm will find that sequence. If the algorithm fails, no manipulation will succeed — the query is not identifiable from observational data at all. This is the theoretical ceiling of what observational data can ever do. Every quasi-experimental method is a named pattern inside this calculus — RCTs close back-doors by design; DiD closes them by differencing away time-invariant confounders; IV uses the front-door-like structure of an unconfounded instrument.

The front-door — smoking, one more time
Pearl's favorite application of his own calculus: even if Fisher had been right about an unobserved genetic confounder between smoking and cancer, we could still estimate the causal effect, using the front-door criterion. The trick: find a mediator (tar in the lungs) that sits between cause and effect and is not confounded with either.
U confounds the direct smoke→cancer relationship. But U does not touch tar. Tar mediates the full effect. So: estimate (smoke→tar) — unconfounded — and (tar→cancer | smoke) — unconfounded — and chain them. Total causal effect recovered without ever seeing U. This kind of algebraic move is unique to Pearl's framework. No purely statistical method can derive it.
Bridge to Chapter 9
We have reached rung 2. We can intervene, experimentally or synthetically, and estimate causal effects. But rung 3 — the counterfactual, the imagined world where a particular person did not take that particular pill — remains. Pearl devotes Ch. 8 to it, and Ch. 9 to its close cousin, mediation. Gallea closes with Ch. 9's call to embrace uncertainty: even with all these tools, our estimates are ranges, not points. Chapter 9 of this reader holds all three.

Counterfactuals, Mediation & Embracing Uncertainty

The top rung of the ladder — and the humility required to stand there.
PEARL · CH 8 & CH 9 GALLEA · CH 9
"Had Cleopatra's nose been shorter, the whole face of the earth would have changed." — Pascal — epigraph to Pearl, Ch. 8 4.6 collapsed counterfactuals and mediation into a single chapter. That flattens a distinction both Pearl and the legal profession take very seriously. v2 restores mediation's distinct weight, and folds in Gallea's Ch. 9 because — remarkably — the chapter on uncertainty is exactly where the top rung belongs.
1
Counterfactuals — the world that didn't happen

A counterfactual is a statement about what would have happened, contrary to fact. "Had I not taken the aspirin, I would still have a headache." It is the most demanding thing the human mind does with causal knowledge — and the thing that distinguishes us from (today's) machines. Pearl's Ch. 8 proves something remarkable: given a structural causal model (a DAG with functions attached), counterfactual queries can be computed. Not merely argued. Computed.

Two quantities from the legal system
PROBABILITY OF NECESSITY · PN
Given that X happened and Y happened, what is the probability that Y would not have happened had X been absent? This is the courtroom's but-for test. Was the defendant's action necessary for the harm? PN gives a number for this, not a narrative.
PROBABILITY OF SUFFICIENCY · PS
Given that X did not happen and Y did not happen, what is the probability that Y would have happened had X been introduced? The flip side: was the action sufficient for the harm? Together, PN and PS give the court a quantitative handle on questions it has been litigating by analogy for centuries.

Pearl's Ch. 8 opens the courtroom door. The but-for test in tort law asks PN: would the harm have occurred but-for the defendant's act? For centuries courts have wrestled with this by analogy and judicial craft. Pearl's framework gives it a number. The discrimination case Carson v. Bethlehem Steel Corp. (1996) — which Pearl quotes directly — frames the standard as counterfactual: "whether the employer would have taken the same action had the employee been of a different race (age, sex, religion, national origin, etc.) and everything else had been the same." That sentence is rung-3 reasoning, written into U.S. case law, decades before Pearl finished the math for computing it.

2
Mediation — the search for how

Pearl devotes a whole chapter (Ch. 9) to mediation, because the mechanism question — "how does X cause Y?" — is subtly different from the effect question. You can know that a drug lowers heart-attack rates without knowing whether it does so by lowering blood pressure, by reducing cholesterol, or by something else entirely. For drug development, how may matter more than whether.

Total Effect = Natural Direct Effect + Natural Indirect Effectmediation decomposes the total into direct and through-mediator components

The formal move Pearl celebrates is the clean decomposition of the total effect into a Natural Direct Effect (the influence of X on Y that does not flow through the mediator M) and a Natural Indirect Effect (the influence that flows through M). These are counterfactual quantities — they ask what Y would be under X's effect while holding M at some value — and were considered intractable before Pearl's framework put them into closed form. Now they are standard in epidemiology, social science, and AI fairness (where "direct" discrimination vs "indirect" discrimination through proxies maps cleanly onto NDE/NIE).

4.6's Ch. 8 briefly mentioned mediation but did not surface NDE/NIE or the fairness application. v2 restores these — and the fairness angle, in particular, is the most consequential modern use of rung-3 reasoning outside the courtroom.
3
Embracing uncertainty — the slope of enlightenment

Gallea closes the Handbook with a chapter titled "Embracing Uncertainty" — less a technical treatment of confidence intervals than a philosophical close. He recalls a master's student who asked: "How is it possible that such an important concept is so widely misunderstood?" He sketches the full arc — from Mount Stupid (introduced in the Preface), through the valley of despair when the fundamental problem first bites, to the slope of enlightenment as methods accumulate. His closing line lands on evidence as a spectrum: "Causality is not black or white. Causal evidence can be viewed rather on a spectrum, from anecdotal pieces of evidence to perfectly measured causal effects." And a practice: every time you hear, read, or say "therefore" or "because," note it. A causal assumption has been made, and it can be questioned.

The arc of the book — and of any honest causal thinker
MOUNT STUPID
"I can tell causes from effects — obviously." Confidence high, knowledge low.
VALLEY OF DESPAIR
"Oh. Everything I thought I knew about cause and effect was vulnerable to confounders, reverse causation, and selection. I cannot trust any headline." The necessary descent.
SLOPE OF ENLIGHTENMENT
"I have tools now — DAGs, the five-step Mindset, do-calculus, quasi-experiments. Not everything is answerable, but many things are, and I know which." Calibrated confidence.
EXPERT PLATEAU
"My estimates come with error bars. My conclusions come with scope conditions. The honest answer is often a range, or a decision procedure, rather than a number. And that is enough to act on." — This is where Pearl's formalism meets Gallea's practice.

This is, in fact, the top rung handled correctly. Counterfactual reasoning — rung 3 — demands imagination, because it asks about worlds that never were. But the world that did happen came with its own noise. An honest causal thinker must be fluent in two forms of not-knowing: the counterfactual (imagining other worlds) and the statistical (being uncertain about this one). Gallea's Ch. 9 is, quietly, the most important chapter in the book.

Bridge to Chapter 10
We have climbed all three rungs. We have Pearl's theory and Gallea's practice. One chapter remains — a look over the horizon. Pearl's Ch. 10 turns to the question that brought him to causal inference in the first place: can we build machines that reason this way? Can strong AI be given a ladder to climb? Gallea's conclusion turns in a different direction: democratization — can we teach the public to reason this way? The two answers converge.

The Causal Revolution — Big Data, AI & What Comes Next

Strong AI, transportability, democratization — why the revolution has barely begun.
PEARL · CH 10 GALLEA · CONCLUSION
"All is pre-determined, yet permission is always granted." — Maimonides — epigraph to Pearl, Ch. 10 4.6 ended on a synthetic "Manifesto" chapter that did not directly map to either book. v2 returns to both authors' actual closing chapters, which turn out to converge on the same destination by completely different routes — and the convergence is the pay-off of reading them together.
1
Pearl's return — can machines climb the ladder?

Pearl came to causal inference from artificial intelligence. He closes the book by going back. The argument is simple and radical: strong AI — machines that think like humans — will require mastery of all three rungs. And deep learning, for all its achievements, has mastered only the first.

What a rung-3 machine could do
EXPLAIN
Not just predict, but tell you why it predicted what it did — in causal terms humans can follow and contest.
ADAPT
When the environment changes, a rung-3 system reasons from its causal model to the new situation. A rung-1 system has to be retrained from scratch. This is the transportability result developed by Elias Bareinboim (Pearl's former student, now a colleague) and Pearl himself in the 2010s: formal conditions under which knowledge from one setting remains valid in another — with the causal diagram marking exactly where the two settings differ.
REFLECT
A counterfactual-capable machine can evaluate its own mistakes: had I acted differently, would the outcome have differed? This is the basis for moral reasoning, regret, and responsibility — each of which, Pearl argues, we will eventually want machines to handle.

Pearl's diagnosis of big-data triumphalism is sharper than Gallea's, because it comes from inside the AI tradition. More data does not climb rungs. Deeper nets do not climb rungs. Interpretability will not climb rungs. Only causal structure — domain knowledge, encoded as assumptions, married to data — climbs them. And domain knowledge, fundamentally, must come from somewhere outside the data. At present that somewhere is a human mind.

2
Gallea's close — democratization

Gallea closes facing the other direction. If Pearl's worry is will AI ever learn this?, Gallea's worry is will the public ever learn this? — because the public is already deciding whether AI products work, whether climate policies work, whether drugs work, every day. The stakes of collective causal illiteracy are huge and currently uninsured.

A manifesto, drawn from both authors
Every important decision is a causal decision. If you cannot articulate the causal claim, you are making the decision in the dark.
Data are dumb; models are indispensable. The structure is not in the data. It is brought to the data. Always.
Draw the diagram. If you cannot draw the DAG, you cannot articulate the claim. The discipline of drawing is the discipline of thinking.
Ask Gallea's five questions. Proxy counterfactual. Something else. Direction of bias. Refine. Extrapolate. Run them on anything anyone tells you.
Use the right tool for the rung. ML for prediction. Causal inference for intervention. Counterfactuals for explanation, responsibility, learning from the past.
Embrace uncertainty. An honest range beats a dishonest point. An acknowledged scope condition beats a pretended universality.
You are smarter than your data. … In the age of computers, this new understanding also brings with it the prospect of amplifying our innate abilities so that we can make better sense of data, be it big or small. — Pearl, final paragraph of the Introduction

Pearl ends the book with that line. Gallea ends the Handbook with a parallel injunction: "Do not keep this knowledge for yourself. Share it." His suggested practice is austere and demanding: every time you hear, read, or say the word therefore or because, note it — a causal claim has just been made and can be questioned. Two authors, two very different books; one destination.

Pearl vs Rubin — a tension this reader doesn't surface Notes p.459
✓ added as context in v2.2. The reader above presents Pearl's framework as the language of modern causal inference.
